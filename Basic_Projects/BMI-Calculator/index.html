What Is BMI?

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a simple, widely used measure that helps you understand whether your weight is in a healthy range for your height. It was developed in the early 19th century by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet and remains one of the most common screening tools used by doctors and health organizations worldwide.

BMI does not measure body fat directly — instead, it uses a mathematical formula based on your weight and height to estimate whether you fall into a category that could indicate health risks. While it is not perfect (it doesn't account for muscle mass, bone density, or fat distribution), it provides a useful starting point for assessing your overall health.

How Is BMI Calculated?

The BMI formula is straightforward. For metric units, you divide your weight in kilograms by the square of your height in meters:

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²

For imperial units, multiply your weight in pounds by 703, then divide by the square of your height in inches:

BMI = (weight (lbs) × 703) ÷ height (in)²

Our free BMI calculator handles both unit systems automatically — just toggle between Metric and Imperial at the top of the calculator.

BMI Categories Explained

The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies BMI results into four main categories. Here's what each one means:

Category BMI Range
Underweight Below 18.5
Normal weight 18.5 – 24.9
Overweight 25.0 – 29.9
Obese 30.0 and above

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is a helpful screening tool, it has some limitations. It does not distinguish between muscle and fat, so athletes or people with high muscle mass may have a high BMI without being overweight. It also does not account for age, gender, ethnicity, or where body fat is stored (belly fat, for example, carries more health risk than fat stored in the hips).

For a more complete picture of your health, consider combining your BMI result with other measurements like waist circumference, body fat percentage, or a consultation with a healthcare professional.
